About Helvetica

One of the most frequently used and recognizable fonts in the world was developed in 1957 by the Swiss designer of fonts Max Miedinger with input from of Edward Hoffman. It was originally called Neue Haas Grotesque.

It all started with the fact that in the 1950s the Swiss company Haas’sche Schriftgiesserei ordered Max Miedinger to remake the existing Haas Grotesque font. Miedinger, along with Edward Hoffman, gave the font a new face, more harmonious with the spirit of the times. The new font was named Neue Haas Grotesque. But later, according to marketing considerations, it was renamed Helvetica, that in translation from Latin means "Swiss".

In the 40s of the 20th century, the so-called Swiss style was born, which cultivated clarity, accuracy and objectivity. Helveticа has become the dominant font for many adherents of this style. For a long time Helveticа was the most commonly used grotesque and the most popular typeface. In connection with this popularity in 1983, Linotype released its improved version, called Helvetica Neue, consisting of an incredible number of variants (51 types), including ultra-thin and super-greasy ones. 

With the introduction of computer desktop publishing systems, Helveticа became a familiar name for every home computer owner, since this font, along with Times, was built into most operating systems, text editors and layout software, from the simplest to professional graphics packages. At the same time Helveticа continues to be widely used in typography and advertising.

The incredible popularity of this font contributed to the fact that Microsoft ordered the firm Monotype grotesque of a close figure, the saturation and width of the signs of which coincide with the signs of Helveticа. This font was called Arial. Thus, the font that we now find in any Microsoft product was designed to replace Helveticа. Many other companies, such as Agfa / Compugraphic, Autologic, Bitretham and Corel, followed the example of Microsoft and ordered their own versions of Helveticа. So the fonts appeared: Helios, Geneva, Swiss 721, Switzerland, Europa Grotesk, Megaron, etc.


Currently Helvetica belongs to the company Lynotype, which in 2001 released an updated and expanded version of the font with a large number of characters, including mathematical symbols, pseudo-graphic glyphs and symbols belonging to various non-Latin alphabets. The total number of characters included in the updated font is 1866. Font supports at least 33 languages, including Latin, Cyrillic, Greek, Japanese, Hebrew, Korean, as well as Vietnamese, Urdu, Hindi and Khmer.

Famous brands using Helvetica

Helveticа is widely used in the logos of various companies. In particular, this font is used by such companies as: 3M, AGFA, AT&T, BASF, Bayer, Blaupunkt, BMW, Energizer, GM, Google, Husqvarna, Intel, Jeep, Lufthansa, Motorola, Nestle, Olympus, Panasonic, Placebo, Stimorol, Samsung, Sanyo, Texaco, Toyota. 

Helvetica clones

In the photo print market of the 1960s and 1970s, a large number of fonts appeared, which were copies of Helveticа. Some of them were direct clones, while others had subtle differences. Among the most famous are: Proxima Nova, Brandon-Grotesque, Akzidenz-Grotesque, Geneva, CNN Sans and etc.
